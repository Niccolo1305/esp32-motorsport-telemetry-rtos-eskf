Internal R&D Report — Addendum to MPU6886_ThermalDrift_Report.pdf

SensorMPU6886 (6-axis MEMS IMU, no on-chip temp compensation)
PlatformM5Stack AtomS3 (ESP32-S3 + 0.85" IPS display)
Test IDtel_94
Firmwarev1.2.2-diag-kgs
Data frameChip-frame (K_gs disabled, rotate_3d disabled for gyro)
Duration (lap=1)2100s (35.0 min)
Total samples104,984 at 50 Hz
Temperature range37.6 – 39.6°C
Date2026-04-11

This report characterizes non-thermal bias drift in the MPU-6886 MEMS gyroscope. A 6-face static test was performed with K_gs G-sensitivity correction and geometric rotation (rotate_3d) disabled in the firmware, so that raw chip-frame gyro and accelerometer data are recorded directly. The sensor was physically rotated to 6 different orientations and held stationary on each face for approximately 5 minutes, taped to a flat surface. These physical rotations appear as brief high-amplitude spikes in the gyroscope plots (clipped at ±2 °/s) and are excluded from the analysis.

1. Experimental Setup & Methodology
The test was performed with firmware v1.2.2-diag-kgs, a diagnostic build with three modifications:

K_gs G-sensitivity correction disabled — the 3×3 matrix that compensates gyroscope sensitivity to gravitational orientation was commented out.
Gyro rotate_3d bypassed — gyroscope output remains in chip-native frame (not rotated to vehicle frame). This ensures raw_gx/gy/gz in the CSV are true chip-frame values.
raw_ax/ay/az = chip-frame accel — instead of linear acceleration (gravity removed), the raw accel fields contain ellipsoid-calibrated chip-frame accelerometer data with gravity.

The sensor was placed on 6 different faces in sequence, each taped to a flat surface for ~5 minutes. The physical rotations between faces were performed manually and take a few seconds each. These transitions produce large gyroscope spikes (tens of °/s) that are clipped in all plots at ±2 °/s to preserve the scale of the much smaller bias drift signal (~0.1–2 °/s).
A warm-up phase (lap=0, ~48 minutes) preceded the test to allow the sensor to reach thermal equilibrium. The 6-face test data is recorded as lap=1.
Face detection: rolling std of |a| over a 2s window, threshold 0.015 g. The 6 longest static segments are selected. First and last 30s of each face are trimmed. Statistics use 5% trimmed means.
2. Warm-up Phase
The sensor was powered on and left stationary for 48 minutes before starting the 6-face test. Temperature rose from 38.8°C to 39.1°C during this phase, after which it stabilized.

6. K_gs Validation
The K_gs G-sensitivity matrix (computed from earlier tests tel_gsen4 + tel_82) was applied offline to the tel_94 chip-frame data. If K_gs correctly removes gravity dependence, the corrected inter-face spread should decrease.

AxisRaw spread (°/s)Corrected spread (°/s)ChangeVerdict
GX2.54493.4455+35.4%WORSE
GY1.16411.6050+37.9%WORSE
GZ0.08750.3629+314.8%WORSE

K_gs correction increases spread on all three axes. This happens because the dominant source of inter-face variation is temporal bias drift, not G-sensitivity. Since the faces were tested sequentially, time and orientation are confounded — K_gs subtracts a gravity-dependent correction that partially correlates with the time trend, making the result worse.
K_gs was removed from firmware in v1.3.0. The non-thermal drift dominates the G-sensitivity signal, making any static gravity correction unreliable.
7. Comparison with Thermal Drift Report
Previous finding (MPU6886_ThermalDrift_Report.pdf)

Gyroscope bias drift was attributed primarily to thermal sensitivity.
The thermal sensitivity dG/dT was found non-repeatable between sessions on GX and GY (inter-run σ ~ 0.10 °/s/°C).
LUT and Poly-3 compensation both failed: 44% of GX samples fell in the "unusable" category even after correction.
Conclusion: no fixed compensation model can capture the non-deterministic drift shape. ZARU is required at every power-on.

New finding (this report)

Temperature was nearly flat during the test (37.6 – 39.6°C, ΔT < 2°C).
Despite flat temperature, significant bias drift was observed:GX: -0.0789 °/s/min (total -2.50 °/s over 1900s)GY: -0.0312 °/s/min (total -0.99 °/s)GZ: +0.0020 °/s/min (stable)
The drift is monotonic over 35 minutes, consistent with electronic bias instability (stress relaxation, charge trapping), not thermal.

Reconciliation
Both effects coexist in the MPU-6886:

Thermal sensitivity — real, observed in the heating tests. The bias changes when temperature changes.
Electronic drift — newly quantified in this test. The bias changes over time even at constant temperature.

The thermal drift report could not separate these two effects because temperature and time were monotonically correlated in all 7 test runs (continuous heating). The "inter-run shape variability" attributed to non-repeatable thermal sensitivity was likely contaminated by the electronic drift component.
ZARU addresses both: it periodically resets the bias estimate at stationarity, regardless of whether the drift source is thermal or electronic.
8. Conclusions

GX has the largest non-thermal drift at -0.0789 °/s/min (4.7 °/s/hour). Over a 10-minute driving stint without stationarity, this accumulates ~0.79 °/s of uncorrected bias error.
GY drift is moderate at -0.0312 °/s/min (1.9 °/s/hour). Less critical: gy (pitch rate) does not directly feed the 2D ESKF heading.
GZ is stable at +0.0020 °/s/min (0.12 °/s/hour). This is excellent for the ESKF, which uses gz for heading estimation.
K_gs correction is counterproductive. Spread after correction: GX +35%, GY +38%, GZ +315% (all worse). K_gs was removed from firmware in v1.3.0.
ZARU remains the primary defense against both thermal and electronic drift. The 3-axis ZARU (v1.2.1) resets bias at stationarity and during straight-line driving, handling both sources transparently.
Recommendation for future calibration: if K_gs is ever revisited, randomize face order (not sequential) to decorrelate temporal drift from gravity orientation.

The MPU-6886 gyroscope exhibits significant non-thermal electronic drift on GX and GY. This drift was previously conflated with thermal sensitivity. ZARU is confirmed as the correct approach — it compensates both drift sources without requiring a temperature model.
